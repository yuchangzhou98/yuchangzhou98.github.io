Redesigning Cornell Blog Theme
My Role

UX & Web Designer

Timeline

Jun 2019 - August 2019

Company

Cornell IT

Tools

Sketch, Adobe XD, Figma, Docker, Pantheon, Lando, Wordpress

In the summer of 2019, I worked at Cornell IT as UX & Web Design Intern. The primary project during my internship is the redesign of Cornell blog theme.

Cornell blog service maintains a WordPress infrastructure that allows Cornell affiliate to put up websites. While the blog service has been widely used by over 300 Cornell affiliated departments, organizations and clubs, it offers only one Cornell branded theme to choose from.

The current theme, last updated in 2017, is very limited in types of widgets and functionalities, which makes it unable to cater to needs of organizations and clubs.

Therefore, we decided to tackle this problem by:

Redesigning the Cornell Branded theme based on users' need, to provide more functionalities and visual design options.

Project Overview

Bug Fixes

Fixing the 2017 Cornell Theme

Since the 2017 Cornell branded theme is having problems in both design and implementation, the first task of our team is to fix all the technical bugs existing in the current version. By adding every available widgets, moving around pages, checking evert possible types of content that could be displayed, especially some corner cases, we were able to compile a list of all technical bugs identified.

After presenting them to our superviors, we spent a week fixing the most important bugs and documenting the blocks and issues we are not able to solve. Most of the unsolvable bugs are caused by the default Wordpress template that does not allow much flexibility. Therefore, we decided to directly replace it in the later implementation.

During the progress of debugging, we also explored the user interactions and recorded the user journey for further study.

User Research

Analysis of Use Cases

We began our design process through an assessment of use cases. Throughout the assessment process, we did research on the uses for cornell.blogs.edu, widget use, and types of content presented. This allowed us to identify prominent user needs.

The three predominant purposes of Cornell Blog Service are:

  • News/Blog - 27.5%
  • Research - 27.5%
  • Program & Organizations - 25%

While 80% of uses cases fall in the above three categories, the other 20% are used for the following contents:

  • Student Resource - 7.5%
  • Course - 5.0%
  • Professor - 5.0%
  • Club - 2.5%

We picked some of the use cases for more in-depth study by contacting the owners of those cases for user interviews to better understand the existing painpoints and needs. and thoroughly investigate the types of features, widgets, and existing limitation in their current webpages.

Painpoints

  • Few options for customization
    While the content of the theme is highly customizable, the theme has very few options for layout and widget styles, leaving all sites to look like a generic Cornell site. Too much constraint in layout and widget styles also leads to hesitation of users.
  • Classic but outdated look
    Users want a more modern look to showcase their content, especially the sport clubs which target young audience. Still, they would like the new design to keep the branding and design language of Cornell.
  • Lacking necessary functionalities
    70% of observed blogs.cornell.edu used a people section, which is manually intensive to self-build. Also, the current theme lacks functionalities like gallery, which is needed by research labs, programs and clubs.

In conclusion, the current theme is lacking certain functionalities and visual design variety.
We set our goal based on this problem statement, aiming to solve the problem.

The Goal

To design a robust Cornell Blog theme with a refreshing visual look while conforming to Cornell's Branding Guidelines and Web Accessibility Guidelines.
