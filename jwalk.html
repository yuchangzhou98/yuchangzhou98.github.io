J-Walk

Design an educational game to motivate language learning.
My Role

Design Lead

Project Team

Cornell Independent Research
Alan, Ziyad, Luna, Yuchang

Timeline

Jan 2018 - April 2019

Tools

Figma, Sketch, Miro, InVision, Photoshop, Illustrator

J-Walk is an educational game based on real-time location to create an engaging language learning experience.

The user can wander around and pick up “words” that are generated from a real world context. In addition, the user can use these collected words to review the words to memorize them and level up or solve language challenges embedded in the same world.

Project Overview

Getting Started

Understanding the problem

"Though I am very interested in Japanese, I have never kept learning vocabulary on app for more than a week..."

Being multi-lingual speakers and passionate language learners, we noticed the phenomena where it is always difficult for beginners to persist on learning new languages, especially when they are no longer taking language classes and try self-teaching on online platform or mobile app instead.

What is the reason behind?

Considering the adequate amount of resources, language learning apps were once believed by experts that they could provide fruitful and efficient learning experience no less than professional institutions. However, most users of these applications have reported one common problem in their uninstallation feedback - we are lacking motivation.

By talking with self-taught language learners and exploring the available apps on the market, I realized that users are lacking motivations because of the following reasons:

1. Tedious learning experience
2. No/weak reward system, no sense of accomplishment
3. Feeling of isolation from peer learners

The Goal/Objective

To enrich learning experience on mobile app by providing strong incentive and interactive study environment.

To achieve the goal, I am looking for answers to the following questions:

  • How could we add additional incentive to the users?
  • How could we get people to feel connected with other users?
  • How could we make the learning experience more interesting?
  • How could we increase user's dependency on the app?
User Research

Interviews

Before I start exploring potential solutions, it is essential to learn about our potential users, therefore I conducted user interviews with individuals who had experience engaging with language learning mobile apps, asking for their experience, opinions and expectation about language learning product. To be more specific, my objective is to:

(1) Learn about users’ need and experience with the app
(2) Uncover pain points behind lack of motivation
(3) Discover potential product opportunities

Selection of Interview Notes

Affinity Diagram

After the interview, I went through the interview notes and sorted the result into categories using affinity diagram. From my observation of the diagram, I noticed users are severely bothered by:

Endlessly accumulating and repetitive study tasks
Over-frequent notifications
Un-personalized study content

While it is easier for users with clear target (exam, diploma test) to persist, users who learn out of flexible demand (travel, interest) requires more interesting learning option and better sharing experience.

Competitive Analysis

I explored some of the most commonly used language learning apps like Duolingo, Drops to better understand the strategies used to create motivation and feeling of freshness.

Takeaways
  • Vocabulary categorized by theme/purpose
  • Reward Mechanism (points, unlock costumes, etc)
  • Variety in challenge design
  • Personal Profile with list of vocabulary learnt
Brainstorming and Ideation

Solution Exploration

During brainstorming session with the entire team, we decided on taking inspiration from video/mobile games, a process coined as "gamification". There are two reasons why we consider gamification as a potential solution:

1. The affinity diagram shows users are desiring for more interesting feature and more variety in tasks.
2. There is large overlap between language learners and game players, as teenagers and young adults playing an important role.

It is therefore feasible that mapping game design elements from onto a non-game context like language learning might yield favorable results.

Idea 1: Challenge with AI or Friends

Users challenge AI or friends who are using this app, earn score or rewards after winning each combat.

Positives

1. Strong social connection with people (friends, learning partners).
2. Learning motives strengthened by competitions.

Negatives

1. Failing to largely improve or change the way of learning new words.
2. Unfriendly to people who do not like competitions.

Idea 2: Collecting Words On Map

Users collect and learn items linked to new words on map, they have to collect all the words in one map and pass the test in order to proceed to the next level (map). Their performance is measured by 1-3 stars based on correctness. Multiple attempts are allowed.

Positives

1. Creating contextual language learning experience.
2. Allowing users to use outdoor spare time, e.g., while having a walk, queueing for coffee, etc.

Negatives

1. Highly dependent on user's outdoor activities.
2. Hard to keep a regular routine for language learning period.

Idea 3: Pet Nursing

Users nurse a pet and learn new words to feed the pet.

Positives

1. Adding additional motivation to learn vocabulary by creating emotion bond between users and virtual pet.
2. Encouraging a regular routine for language learning time period by feeding mechanism.

Negatives

1. Failing to largely improve or change the way of learning new words.

User Flow

We took Idea 2: Collecting Words on Map as our major approach, in order to test whether contextual language learning could boost the learning efficiency and strengthen the duration one new word stays in people's mind.
Therefore, we decided to build one functional MVP (minimum viable product) with the following functions for A/B testing:

  • 1. Collecting new words on map
  • 2. Reviewing new words with quizes
  • 3. Viewing all words learnt in a list
  • 4. Settings to choose walk/still mode

A/B Testing

I conducted a user study with a total of 59 participants recruited from the university community. There was no re-quirement to participate in the study besides being at least eighteen years of age. An individual session with a participant took approximately 45 minutes to an hour, and the study took place over the course of two days.

Participants were assigned to two groups of equal size:
Group A would stay still while playing (still mode)
Group B would walk around campus to pick up new words and learn.

Before and after 30-min playing, every participant would complete pre- and post-test in which they would match eight Japanese Words to their English definitions. I chose words that could be found in the area where the study took place.

Key Findings
  • Contextual language learning is helpful for short-term memory.
    While both groups have similar performance in pre-test, Group A (still mode) improved 3.6 as mean, and Group B (walking mode) raised 5.2 in their performance as average.
  • The review system needed to be more tightly integrated into the core gameplay.
    “There should be more motivation to stop exploring and review. It's very easy to overwhelm yourself with vocabulary without reviewing.”
  • Players enjoyed walking and indicated that they would play the game while walking during their everyday life.
    “It's something that I can see myself doing on my walk to class... I can just build [it] into my routine if I wanted to...I would probably use this as much as [Duolingo].”

High-Fidelity Prototype

Based on usability testing, I iterated the overall user flow, and designed the High-Fidelity Prototype for J-Walk.

Learn

The primary interface of J-Walk is an interactive map of the player's immediate surroundings.

Users could click on sushi icons within their range (blue circle), and learn the word poped up.

Words are generated related to the corresponding venue. In this example, users get Japanese word "本" (book) as they click Olin Library on the map.

Challenge

Users could start challenge by clicking stars within their range. Challenges would consist of various type of quizzes, including sentence completion, matching, etc.

Stars earned could be used to exchange for unique costumes and unlock achievements.

Review

Users could choose specific category to review the words they have learnt.

Experience points boost when users finished one round of review.

More Features

Play
Users could interact with their pet in this module.

Decorations, items and themes could be earned by collecting sushis and stars during exploration.

Connect
Users could send battle invite to nearby J-Walk players.

Both players would be given a series of word quizzes and medals would be rewarded to the winner.

Reflection

J-Walk is one of the works I am most proud of throughout my UX design experience.

It is not exaggerated to say that this project gives me so much inspiration, that I finally decided to choose UX design as my career. Since we aim to use game play to facilitate language learning experience, I conducted 4 rounds of usability testing and design iterations in order to find balance between game app and educational app.

Moreover, as the design lead, I am responsible for leading the brainstorming session and weekly design updates for the entire team, from which I largely improved my problem solving and communication skills.

Limitations
  • Lacking Long-term Usability Testing Results
    Although J-Walk is designed to sustain long-term engagement, participants only played for a short time in our current study (less than an hour). Therefore, we do not yet know the effects of J-Walk on long-term engagement or long-term retention of learned vocabulary.
  • Needing Consideration for Vocabulary of Uncommon Categories
    While contextual language learning works effectively in strengthening user's memory of words, the algorithm which binds word with venues has its drawback in unevenness of words' appearance.
    Words of uncommon categories have lower chance to be generated during the game play process, because of the low appearance rate of related locations. For example, "crime" and "prison" would need users to be physically near prisons or courts, however these locations are hard to encounter if compared with restuarants or supermarkets.
Moving Forward

In the future, I would like conduct a study where participants play the game over the course of weeks or months.

In addition, I plan to incorporate elements into game that promote long-term retention and engagement, such as backing the review system with the Leitner system.

Thank you to Alan, Ziyad and Luna for the amazing programming work bringing the prototype into one functional product. I am excited to see more growth in J-Walk as an eductional game app and hopefully it could impact the language learning community in a creative and helpful way.
