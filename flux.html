Project Overview

What is Flux?

Flux is a crowd tracking app that measures real-time occupancy of facilities on Cornell campus. The mission of our product is to help students make smart decisions based on Flux's instant crowding report.

The primary goal for Flux's team in Spring 2020 is to include Cornell fitness centers in our scope.

Therefore, as the product designer, I am responsible for designing the user flow and interfaces that communicate real-time data with users in a easily-comprehensible way.

Team & My Role

My work includes defining the vision and product strategy, conducting three rounds of user studies, and designing from scratch to fully-functional prototypes (Home screen, Detail Page, Profile) with one co-designer.

In addition, I collaborated with 1 Co-designer, 1 Product Manager and 5 Developers by managing the entire design lifecycle in bringing out the product live.

Context

What is The Problem we are trying to solve?

As you may already know, Cornell is HUGE.

According to the latest statistics posted on its Official Website, Cornell has a total student enrollment of 24,027 along with 10000+ faculty and staff. However, comparing to this significant population, Cornell has only 5 fitness centers on campus, with a total capacity of 346 people.

  • North (Appel+Helen Newman) Capacity: 146
  • Central (Teagle Up+Down) Capacity: 128
  • West (Noyes) Capacity: 72

Unsurprisingly, gyms are often times overly crowded, especially during evenings and weekends.

It's hard to get great workout from a busy gym. (via Awful Gear)

Moreover, Cornell's fitness centers are far from one another, as they are sparsely spreaded across North, West and Central campus. As estimated by Google Map, it takes at minimum 14 minutes to walk from one gym site to another. The distance, along with the harsh weather (Cornell snows for 7 months!) and the mountainous topography makes it impossible for most people to switch location after arriving at one crowded gym.

Thus, the only choice left for them is ‚Äî Waiting.

Figure 1: Fitness sites (labeled with red pin) are at least 14 min. apart from one another.

Students keep complaining about the wasted time, noises and inconveniency caused by unpredictable crowding in fitness centers.

"If the gym looks full, I would just leave and give up my work out plan."

Being aware of the existing problem, our team decides to take initiative and explore possible solutions.

We quickly realize that Flux, which is by then only tracking dining halls' occupancy, could potentially help students make better work-out schedule by including fitness centers in its crowding report.

However, we are still unsure about the following questions:

1. Who are our target users?
2. What information are users looking for?
3. How could we improve the overall work-out experience with Flux?

Getting a Clearer Picture

User Interviews - Inquiring users for valuable insights

I partnered with my co-designer Katie and PM Yvonne to explore students' experience with Cornell Fitness Centers. We randomly picked 15 people at Noyes fitness center and asked a few questions with regard to their work-out routine on weekly basis at fitness centers.

Figure 2: General Questions for User Interview

Answer to Question 1: Categorizing our user group

The target users of Flux Fitness typically fall somewhere between this Workout Spectrum based on their workout habit. We categorized them into three classes (Casual, Mid-Level, Power) and summarised the feature level insights initially from each user group, as shown in the figure below.

Figure 3: Users Breakdown

Other Key Insights

Other than the feature oriented insights there were few other common attributes identified from the users.

Prefer symbols and colors rather than text.
Users appreciate the current color representation for occupancy level, and would like to have more simplified text information.

Desire a lightweight experience.
No clutter and unwanted elements in the product where they can finish their task and search for results quickly.

Personalization needed.
Users expressed their hope in personalization of the product. They expect the product to learn their habits and send notification when their liked place is free.

Answering Question 2: Identify necessary information

We analyzed our users' needs and categorized data into Primary Information and Secondary Information.

Aiming to provide a clear overview of gyms based on occupancy, we decided to use a layout of data cards for seperate fitness center with the most important info (name, overall availability, opening status) . Availability breakdown, historical graph and detailed equipment list are put into Detail Screen.

How I collaborate with co-designer, developers and PM

We (Katie and I) are assigned to be responsible for separate tasks, and synchronize with each other every half week during the design workshop, where our PM Yvonne would also give opinions and suggestions.

Then, after we made temporary decisions, we update our developers with the changes at the weekly team meeting, and make final adjustments on our design.

In this way, the entire team are well-informed about any decisions made on our product.

How I deal with blocks

Our project team, DTI, short for Cornell Design & Tech Initiative, has a group of amazingly talented designers.

If Katie and I encounter with some block where we could hardly find an effective design for, we present on our weekly design critique and consult with designers on other sub-teams. For most of the times, our design fellows would give super-helpful insights which could help us make decisions. For very rare cases, if our fellow designers are also unsure about the solution, we would do a brief user testing and compare the feedbacks.
